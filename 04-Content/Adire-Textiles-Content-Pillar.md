## What Adire is (in one paragraph)

Adire is an indigo resist-dye textile tradition originating with the Yoruba people of southwest Nigeria, primarily in Abeokuta and Ibadan. The fabric is dyed with locally cultivated indigo and resist-patterned by one of three methods: cassava-paste hand-painting (*eleko*), stitch-resist (*alabere*), and tie-resist (*oniko*). Modern Adire continues this lineage; "Adire-inspired" refers to textiles that pull the aesthetic but are not produced in this tradition. Both have a place in the brand — but they must be named correctly. See [[Cultural-Care-Guidelines]].

## Strategic role

## Voice notes

- Always specify Yoruba/Nigerian origin when naming Adire directly.
- Always distinguish authentic vs inspired in shoppable copy.
- Lead with the **dye process** when describing — it's what makes it Adire.
- Avoid: "ancient symbol of [X]," "tribal print," "ethnic textile," "African batik" (Adire is not batik).

## Forbidden

- Conflating Adire with mudcloth (Bògòlanfini, Mali), kente (Akan, Ghana), or kuba cloth (DR Congo).
- Calling generic shibori or batik "Adire."
- "Tribal" anything.
- Authenticity claims on AI-generated images.
- "Ancient African symbol" without a citation.
