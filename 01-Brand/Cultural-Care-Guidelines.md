# Cultural-Care Guidelines

Ochre & Chrome trades in aesthetic territory — Afrohemian, Adire, African-inspired craft — that has real cultural origins. Treating that territory as a "look" is the fastest way to break audience trust and to attract justified backlash. These rules are non-negotiable for any agent producing copy or visuals.

## The bright lines

**Never:**
- Use "tribal" as a generic adjective. ("Tribal print," "tribal pattern," "tribal vibes" — all banned.)
- Use "ethnic" as an aesthetic descriptor for a product or room.
- Invent cultural origin claims ("ancient African symbol of fertility," "traditional shamanic motif"). If we don't have a documented source, we don't claim one.
- Call AI-generated visuals "authentic."
- Use "boho-chic." Use **Afrohemian**.
- Conflate distinct traditions. Adire (Yoruba, Nigeria) is not Mudcloth/Bògòlanfini (Bamana, Mali). Kente (Akan, Ghana) is not Kuba (DR Congo). Specificity is care.

**Always:**
- Default to **"African-inspired"** unless we know the maker, region, and tradition.
- Use **"artisan-made"** only when we can name the artisan, cooperative, or country of origin.
- Use **"Adire-inspired"** for visuals/products that pull the indigo-resist aesthetic but are not documented Yoruba textiles. Reserve **"Adire"** (no qualifier) for documented Nigerian pieces.
- Frame Adire, when named, as a **Yoruba indigo resist-dye textile tradition from Nigeria**.
- Distinguish three product tiers in copy:
  1. **Authentic artisan-made** — sourced from named maker / cooperative / 54kibo / Etsy artisans with documented provenance. Highest copy claim.
  2. **Inspired by** — designed in the aesthetic tradition but not from the originating region. Use "[X]-inspired."
  3. **Generic decor** — visually adjacent but unrelated. Don't market it as cultural.
- Credit makers and regions when we have them. "Hand-dyed by Yoruba artisans in Abeokuta" beats "Beautiful African pillow."

## Approved language palette

| Use | Avoid |
|---|---|
| African-inspired | African-style |
| Artisan-made | Handcrafted by tribesmen |
| Adire-inspired indigo textile | Tribal print pillow |
| Yoruba indigo resist-dye textile | Ancient African symbol |
| Hand-loomed natural fiber | Ethnic weave |
| Globally collected interior | Worldly tribal vibe |
| Textile-led, layered, soulful | Boho-chic, gypsy, exotic |
| Mudcloth (when documented Bògòlanfini) | "African mudcloth pattern" (when generic) |
| Kuba cloth (when documented) | "African geometric textile" (when generic) |
| Carved wood stool from [region] | Tribal stool |

## The AI-image rule

A Midjourney render of an "Adire pillow on a sofa" is **not** an Adire pillow. It is an *image of an indigo-resist-style textile*. The pin caption must reflect that:

- ✅ "An Adire-inspired indigo textile, layered onto a warm linen sofa."
- ❌ "Authentic Adire pillow."

When the pin links to a product page that *does* sell a documented Adire piece, the pin can name it directly. When it links to a roundup, it must distinguish authentic vs inspired in the copy.

## Affiliate-product carve-out

When recommending products, separate the two tiers explicitly:

> **Authentic** — *Sourced from Yoruba artisans via 54kibo. Each piece is hand-dyed in Nigeria.*
>
> **Inspired** — *Mass-market indigo cushion that captures the Adire aesthetic at a lower price point. Not artisan-made; we recommend it as an entry piece.*

Both are valid recommendations. Conflating them is not.

## Tone

Editorial, appreciative, accurate. Not academic, not apologetic, not performatively reverent. Treat African design traditions the way Architectural Digest treats Italian craftsmanship — with specificity and respect.

## When in doubt

Strip the cultural claim and lead with the material. "A hand-loomed indigo textile, beautifully irregular, layered against warm linen" is honest. "An ancient symbol of African heritage" is not — unless we can cite it.
